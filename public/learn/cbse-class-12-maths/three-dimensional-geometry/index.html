Three Dimensional Geometry

This chapter of CBSE Class 12 Mathematics covers 10 concepts, taught bottom-up. The AI tutor builds each from the ground up and checks you understand it before the next. Here's what you'll work through:

  1. Find the direction cosines of a line — Direction cosines l, m, n are the cosines of the angles a directed line makes with the x-, y-, and z-axes; they satisfy l^2 + m^2 + n^2 = 1.
  2. Find the direction ratios of a line — Direction ratios are any three numbers proportional to the direction cosines; normalise by dividing by sqrt(a^2+b^2+c^2) to recover the direction cosines.
  3. Find direction cosines of a line through two points — Get direction ratios from coordinate differences, then normalise by the distance PQ to obtain the direction cosines.
  4. Write the vector equation of a line — A line through point a parallel to vector b is r = a + lambda*b; every point on the line corresponds to exactly one value of the real parameter lambda.
  5. Write the Cartesian equation of a line — The line through (x1,y1,z1) with direction ratios a,b,c satisfies (x-x1)/a = (y-y1)/b = (z-z1)/c; this is obtained by eliminating lambda from the parametric form.
  6. Find the angle between two lines — The acute angle between two lines equals cos^{-1}(|a1*a2 + b1*b2 + c1*c2| / (|b1||b2|)) using their direction vectors.
  7. Test two lines for perpendicularity or parallelism — Two lines are perpendicular if a1*a2 + b1*b2 + c1*c2 = 0, and parallel if their direction ratios are proportional.
  8. Recognise skew lines — Skew lines are non-coplanar lines in space that are neither intersecting nor parallel — a situation that cannot occur in 2D.
  9. Define the shortest distance between two lines — The shortest distance between two lines is the minimum length of any segment with one endpoint on each line; it is zero for intersecting lines and equals the perpendicular distance for parallel lines.
  10. Compute the shortest distance between two skew lines — Use d = |(a2 - a1).(b1 x b2)| / |b1 x b2| to compute the shortest distance between two skew lines r = a1 + lambda*b1 and r = a2 + mu*b2.
