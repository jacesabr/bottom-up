Conic Sections

This chapter of JEE Maths covers 19 concepts, taught bottom-up. The AI tutor builds each from the ground up and checks you understand it before the next. Here's what you'll work through:

  1. Identify a conic section as the intersection of a plane and a cone — Recognise that a conic section is the curve produced when a plane cuts a double-napped right circular cone.
  2. Classify the conic from the angle the cutting plane makes with the axis — Decide whether the section is a circle, ellipse, parabola or hyperbola by comparing beta (plane-axis angle) with alpha (semi-vertical angle) and 90 degrees.
  3. Define a circle by its centre and radius — State that a circle is the locus of points equidistant from a fixed centre, the common distance being the radius.
  4. Write the equation of a circle with given centre and radius — Apply the distance formula to the locus condition to get (x - h)^2 + (y - k)^2 = r^2 for centre (h, k) and radius r.
  5. Find a circle's centre and radius by completing the square — Convert a general second-degree circle equation to standard form by completing the square, then read off the centre and radius.
  6. Define a parabola by its focus and directrix — State that a parabola is the locus of points equidistant from a fixed focus and a fixed directrix line, and name its axis and vertex.
  7. Derive and use the standard equation y^2 = 4ax of a parabola — Obtain y^2 = 4ax from the focus-directrix condition with vertex at the origin, and identify the four standard orientations.
  8. Compute the latus rectum of a parabola as 4a — Define the latus rectum of a parabola and show its length is 4a for y^2 = 4ax.
  9. Define an ellipse by the constant sum of focal distances — State that an ellipse is the locus of points whose distances from two foci add to a constant, and name its centre, major and minor axes and vertices.
  10. Relate the semi-axes and focal distance of an ellipse (a^2 = b^2 + c^2) — Use the lengths 2a, 2b, 2c (major axis, minor axis, distance between foci) and the relation a^2 = b^2 + c^2.
  11. Compute the eccentricity of an ellipse as e = c/a — Define the eccentricity e = c/a of an ellipse and use it to place the foci at distance ae from the centre.
  12. Use the standard equation x^2/a^2 + y^2/b^2 = 1 of an ellipse — Apply the standard equation of an ellipse, read which axis is major from the larger denominator, and extract foci, vertices and axis lengths.
  13. Compute the latus rectum of an ellipse as 2b^2/a — Define the latus rectum of an ellipse and show its length is 2b^2/a.
  14. Define a hyperbola by the constant difference of focal distances — State that a hyperbola is the locus of points whose distances from two foci differ by a constant, and name its centre, transverse and conjugate axes and vertices.
  15. Relate the axes and focal distance of a hyperbola (b^2 = c^2 - a^2) — Use 2c (distance between foci), 2a (transverse axis) and the defining quantity b = sqrt(c^2 - a^2), with 2b the conjugate axis.
  16. Compute the eccentricity of a hyperbola as e = c/a (>= 1) — Define the eccentricity e = c/a of a hyperbola and note it is never less than one, with the foci at distance ae from the centre.
  17. Use the standard equation x^2/a^2 - y^2/b^2 = 1 of a hyperbola — Apply the standard equation of a hyperbola, read the transverse axis from the positive term, and extract foci, vertices and lengths.
  18. Compute the latus rectum of a hyperbola as 2b^2/a — Define the latus rectum of a hyperbola and use its length 2b^2/a.
  19. Derive standard conic equations from their geometric definitions — Turn the focus/directrix or distance-sum/difference definition of any conic into its standard equation and read off its key features.
