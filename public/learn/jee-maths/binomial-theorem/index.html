Binomial Theorem

This chapter of JEE Maths covers 13 concepts, taught bottom-up. The AI tutor builds each from the ground up and checks you understand it before the next. Here's what you'll work through:

  1. Prove a statement for all natural numbers by mathematical induction — Knock over an infinite line of dominoes with two facts: the first one falls, and each falling domino topples the next — this is the induction engine that later powers the proof of the binomial theorem.
  2. Read off the term, power and coefficient patterns in a binomial expansion — From the worked-out expansions of (a+b)^0 through (a+b)^4, recognise the three governing patterns: term count, how the powers of a and b change, and the constant index sum.
  3. Compute the binomial coefficients nCr that fill Pascal's triangle — Rewrite the numbers in Pascal's triangle as combinations nCr = n!/(r!(n-r)!), so any coefficient can be found directly without building earlier rows.
  4. Build Pascal's triangle and use a row to expand a binomial — Generate successive rows of Pascal's triangle by adding adjacent entries, then use the required row together with the power patterns to expand a binomial.
  5. State the binomial theorem for a positive integral index n — Write the general expansion of (a+b)^n with binomial coefficients, in both the term-by-term and sigma-notation forms, and know it is proved by induction.
  6. Expand a given binomial power using the binomial theorem — Apply the theorem to a concrete binomial, substituting each part (including fractions or powers of x) carefully and simplifying every term.
  7. Expand a difference (x - y)^n with alternating signs — Specialise the theorem to b = -y so that the terms alternate in sign, the general term carrying a factor (-1)^r.
  8. Expand (1 + x)^n and derive the sum of all binomial coefficients — Set a = 1, b = x to expand (1+x)^n in terms of nCr, then put x = 1 to show the coefficients add to 2^n.
  9. Expand (1 - x)^n and derive the alternating sum of coefficients — Set a = 1, b = -x to expand (1-x)^n with alternating signs, then put x = 1 to show the alternating coefficient sum is zero.
  10. Evaluate a numerical power by splitting the base for the binomial theorem — Write a number as a convenient sum or difference (e.g. 98 = 100 - 2) and expand its power by the binomial theorem to compute it exactly.
  11. Compare or approximate a power using the first few binomial terms — Expand a power like (1.01)^1000000 with the binomial theorem and keep only the leading terms (all remaining terms positive) to bound or approximate it.
  12. Prove divisibility or remainder results using the binomial theorem — Expand a power of the form (1 + a)^n, factor out a power of a from the higher terms, and read off the divisibility or remainder.
  13. Apply the binomial theorem to expand, evaluate and prove — Use the binomial theorem and its special cases as a single toolkit: expand any binomial power, evaluate numerical powers, compare or approximate, and prove divisibility results.
