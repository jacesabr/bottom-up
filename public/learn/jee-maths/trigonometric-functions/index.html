Trigonometric Functions

This chapter of JEE Maths covers 26 concepts, taught bottom-up. The AI tutor builds each from the ground up and checks you understand it before the next. Here's what you'll work through:

  1. Locate a point in the Cartesian plane by its ordered pair (x, y) — Pin any point in the plane with two perpendicular number lines, reading its position as the ordered pair (x, y) whose signs name the quadrant.
  2. Identify the radius, chord and arc of a circle and use circumference = 2 pi r — Name the parts of a circle — radius, chord, arc — and measure the whole way around it with circumference = 2 pi r, keeping the curved arc distinct from the straight chord.
  3. Apply Pythagoras a^2 + b^2 = c^2 and read off x^2 + y^2 = 1 on the unit circle — Relate the two legs and the hypotenuse of a right triangle by a^2 + b^2 = c^2, and see why a point (x, y) on the unit circle obeys x^2 + y^2 = 1.
  4. Measure an angle by the rotation of a ray and assign its sign — Treat an angle as the amount of rotation of a ray about its initial point, with anticlockwise rotation positive and clockwise rotation negative.
  5. Express an angle in degrees, minutes and seconds — Use the degree as one 360th of a revolution and subdivide it into minutes and seconds (1 deg = 60', 1' = 60'').
  6. Define the radian as the angle subtended by an arc equal to the radius — Define one radian as the angle an arc of length one unit subtends at the centre of a unit circle, and treat radian measures as real numbers.
  7. Relate arc length, radius and central angle by l = r theta — Use l = r theta (theta in radians) to find any one of arc length, radius or central angle from the other two.
  8. Convert between degree and radian measure — Use pi radian = 180 deg to convert any angle between degrees and radians.
  9. Define cos x and sin x as the coordinates of a unit-circle point — For a point P(a, b) on the unit circle at angle x, define cos x = a and sin x = b, extending the trig ratios to any angle.
  10. Establish the identity sin^2 x + cos^2 x = 1 — Use that P(a, b) lies on the unit circle (a^2 + b^2 = 1) to prove cos^2 x + sin^2 x = 1 for every angle x.
  11. Define cosec, sec, tan and cot from sin and cos — Define the other four trig functions as cosec x = 1/sin x, sec x = 1/cos x, tan x = sin x/cos x, cot x = cos x/sin x, with their exclusions.
  12. Derive 1 + tan^2 x = sec^2 x and 1 + cot^2 x = cosec^2 x — Divide sin^2 x + cos^2 x = 1 by cos^2 x and by sin^2 x to obtain the two derived Pythagorean identities.
  13. Determine the sign of a trig function from its quadrant — Use the signs of the coordinates a, b in each quadrant to decide the sign of each trigonometric function there.
  14. State the domain and range of each trigonometric function — Give the domain and range of sin, cos, cosec, sec, tan and cot, including the bound -1 <= sin x, cos x <= 1.
  15. Use the periodicity of trig functions to reduce and sketch them — Apply sin(2n pi + x) = sin x, cos(2n pi + x) = cos x and tan(pi + x) = tan x to reduce large angles and sketch the graphs.
  16. Find all six trig values from one value and the quadrant — Given one trig function and the quadrant, use the identities to find the other five, fixing each sign by the quadrant.
  17. Apply the even-odd identities cos(-x) = cos x, sin(-x) = -sin x — Use that reflecting P across the x-axis sends x to -x and b to -b to get cos(-x) = cos x and sin(-x) = -sin x.
  18. Prove and apply cos(x +/- y) — Establish cos(x + y) = cos x cos y - sin x sin y and cos(x - y) = cos x cos y + sin x sin y, then use them to evaluate angles.
  19. Derive the cofunction and allied-angle identities — Use the cos(x - y) formula to get cos(pi/2 - x) = sin x, sin(pi/2 - x) = cos x and the related allied-angle reductions.
  20. Prove and apply sin(x +/- y) — Establish sin(x + y) = sin x cos y + cos x sin y and sin(x - y) = sin x cos y - cos x sin y, then use them to evaluate angles.
  21. Derive and apply tan(x +/- y) and cot(x +/- y) — Obtain tan(x +/- y) and cot(x +/- y) by dividing the sine and cosine sum/difference formulas, with the stated angle restrictions.
  22. Derive and apply the double-angle identities — Put y = x in the sum formulas to get cos 2x, sin 2x and tan 2x in their several equivalent forms.
  23. Derive and apply the triple-angle identities — Use sin 3x = sin(2x + x) etc. to obtain sin 3x, cos 3x and tan 3x in terms of the single angle.
  24. Apply the sum-to-product identities — Use cos x +/- cos y and sin x +/- sin y written as products of cosines/sines of (x+y)/2 and (x-y)/2.
  25. Apply the product-to-sum identities — Use 2 cos x cos y, -2 sin x sin y, 2 sin x cos y and 2 cos x sin y written as sums/differences of cosines and sines.
  26. Prove and apply trigonometric identities to evaluate and simplify — Combine the unit-circle definitions, the Pythagorean identities and the sum/difference, multiple-angle and product-sum formulas to prove identities and evaluate trigonometric expressions.
