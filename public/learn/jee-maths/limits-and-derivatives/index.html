Limits and Derivatives

This chapter of JEE Maths covers 16 concepts, taught bottom-up. The AI tutor builds each from the ground up and checks you understand it before the next. Here's what you'll work through:

  1. Interpret the derivative as an instantaneous rate and the slope of the tangent — Build the intuitive idea of a derivative: shrink the averaging interval until the average rate of change becomes the instantaneous rate, equal to the slope of the tangent to the curve.
  2. State the limit of a function as the value it should assume at a point — Understand a limit intuitively: as x is taken closer and closer to a, f(x) settles toward a value l, written lim x->a f(x) = l - the value f(x) should assume at x = a.
  3. Use left- and right-hand limits to decide whether a limit exists — Compute the left-hand and right-hand limits separately and conclude the (two-sided) limit exists only when they coincide.
  4. Apply the algebra of limits to sums, differences, products and quotients — Use Theorem 1: when the limits of f and g both exist, the limit distributes over sum, difference, product and quotient (denominator non-zero), with a constant pulling out.
  5. Evaluate the limit of a polynomial by substitution — Use the fact that lim x->a x^n = a^n with the algebra of limits to show a polynomial's limit at a is just its value f(a).
  6. Evaluate limits of rational functions, resolving the 0/0 form by cancelling factors — Evaluate a rational function by substitution; if the result is 0/0, cancel the common (x - a) factors first, and recognise the non-zero-over-zero case where the limit does not exist.
  7. Apply the standard limit (x^n - a^n)/(x - a) = n a^(n-1) — Use Theorem 2: lim x->a (x^n - a^n)/(x - a) = n a^(n-1) (true for any rational n, a > 0) to evaluate limits of difference-quotient type.
  8. Use the Sandwich Theorem to evaluate a squeezed limit — Apply Theorem 4: if f(x) <= g(x) <= h(x) near a and f and h share the same limit l at a, then g also has limit l at a.
  9. Apply the standard trigonometric limits (sin x)/x -> 1 and (1 - cos x)/x -> 0 — Use Theorem 5: lim x->0 (sin x)/x = 1 and lim x->0 (1 - cos x)/x = 0, with the algebra of limits, to evaluate trigonometric limits.
  10. Compute a derivative from the first-principle limit definition — Apply the definition f'(a) = lim h->0 [f(a+h) - f(a)]/h (and f'(x) = lim h->0 [f(x+h) - f(x)]/h) to find derivatives from first principles.
  11. Interpret f'(a) geometrically as the slope of the tangent — Read the first-principle difference quotient as the slope of a chord, and its limit f'(a) as the slope of the tangent to y = f(x) at the point.
  12. Differentiate combinations using the sum, difference, product and quotient rules — Apply the derivative rules: (u +/- v)' = u' +/- v', the product rule (uv)' = u'v + uv', and the quotient rule (u/v)' = (u'v - uv')/v^2.
  13. Differentiate a power x^n using the power rule n x^(n-1) — Apply Theorem 6: the derivative of f(x) = x^n is n x^(n-1) (true for any real power n), derived from first principles via the binomial expansion or induction with the product rule.
  14. Differentiate a polynomial term by term — Use Theorem 7: differentiate a_n x^n + ... + a_1 x + a_0 term by term to get n a_n x^(n-1) + (n-1) a_(n-1) x^(n-2) + ... + a_1.
  15. Differentiate trigonometric functions using first principles and the rules — Establish d/dx(sin x) = cos x and d/dx(tan x) = sec^2 x from first principles, then differentiate other trig functions with the product and quotient rules.
  16. Differentiate polynomial and trigonometric functions by combining limits and derivative rules — Tie the chapter together: from the limit machinery and the first-principle definition, differentiate any polynomial, rational or trigonometric combination using the power, sum, product and quotient rules.
