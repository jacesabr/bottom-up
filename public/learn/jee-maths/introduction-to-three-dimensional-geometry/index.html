Introduction to Three Dimensional Geometry

This chapter of JEE Maths covers 14 concepts, taught bottom-up. The AI tutor builds each from the ground up and checks you understand it before the next. Here's what you'll work through:

  1. Recall the distance between two points in the coordinate plane — A point in the plane is an ordered pair (x, y), and the distance between two such points is sqrt((x2-x1)^2 + (y2-y1)^2).
  2. Recall the Pythagoras theorem and its converse — In a right triangle the square of the hypotenuse equals the sum of the squares of the other two sides; conversely, if the largest side squared equals the sum of the other two squares, the angle opposite it is a right angle.
  3. Set up the three coordinate axes and three coordinate planes in space — To work in space, three mutually perpendicular lines (axes) meeting at a single point (origin) define three coordinate planes and a rectangular coordinate system.
  4. Identify the eight octants the coordinate planes divide space into — Recognise that the three coordinate planes cut space into eight regions called octants, numbered I to VIII.
  5. Associate a point in space with an ordered triplet (x, y, z) — Read off the three coordinates of a point as its perpendicular distances from the three coordinate planes, giving a one-to-one correspondence between points and triplets (x, y, z).
  6. Write the coordinate form of points lying on an axis or a coordinate plane — Use the special forms of coordinates for points that lie on a coordinate axis (one nonzero coordinate) or in a coordinate plane (one zero coordinate).
  7. Determine the octant of a point from the signs of its coordinates — Use the sign of each coordinate, together with the octant sign table, to name the octant in which a given point lies.
  8. Compute the distance between two points using the 3D distance formula — Apply PQ = sqrt((x2-x1)^2 + (y2-y1)^2 + (z2-z1)^2) to find the distance between two points in space.
  9. Find the distance of a point from the origin — Specialise the distance formula to the origin: OQ = sqrt(x^2 + y^2 + z^2).
  10. Test whether three points are collinear using distances — Decide if three points lie on a line by checking whether the largest pairwise distance equals the sum of the other two.
  11. Classify a triangle (right-angled, isosceles) from its side lengths — Use the three side lengths from the distance formula to decide whether a triangle is right-angled (Pythagoras) or isosceles (two equal sides).
  12. Find the equation of a set of points (locus) from a distance condition — Let P be (x, y, z), translate a given distance condition into an equation using the distance formula, and simplify to the equation of the set of points.
  13. Relate a triangle's centroid to its vertices' coordinates — Use that each centroid coordinate is the average of the corresponding coordinates of the three vertices to find a missing vertex or the centroid.
  14. Locate points in space and solve distance problems in three dimensional geometry — Combine the coordinate frame, octants and the distance formula to locate any point in space and to solve problems on collinearity, triangles, loci and centroids.
