Complex Numbers and Quadratic Equations

This chapter of JEE Maths covers 18 concepts, taught bottom-up. The AI tutor builds each from the ground up and checks you understand it before the next. Here's what you'll work through:

  1. Use the imaginary unit i with i^2 = -1 — Introduce i as a symbol for the square root of -1 satisfying i^2 = -1, the device that lets the equation x^2 + 1 = 0 have a solution.
  2. Define a complex number as a + ib — Recognise any number of the form a + ib, with a and b real, as a complex number.
  3. Identify the real part Re z and imaginary part Im z — Read off the real part and imaginary part of z = a + ib as the real numbers a and b.
  4. Apply the equality condition for two complex numbers — Use the rule that two complex numbers are equal exactly when their real parts and imaginary parts separately agree, to solve for unknown reals.
  5. Add and subtract complex numbers component-wise — Add (and subtract) complex numbers by combining real parts with real parts and imaginary parts with imaginary parts.
  6. Multiply two complex numbers using i^2 = -1 — Multiply complex numbers by expanding the product and replacing i^2 by -1, giving (ac - bd) + i(ad + bc).
  7. Evaluate powers of i using the cycle of four — Reduce any integer power of i (positive or negative) using the four-step cycle 1, i, -1, -i.
  8. Find the multiplicative inverse of a non-zero complex number — Compute 1/z for a non-zero z = a + ib using the formula a/(a^2+b^2) + i(-b)/(a^2+b^2).
  9. Divide one complex number by another — Compute the quotient z1/z2 (z2 not zero) as z1 times the inverse of z2, equivalently by multiplying by the conjugate of the denominator.
  10. Express the square root of a negative real number — Write the square root of a negative real number using i: for a > 0, sqrt(-a) = sqrt(a) i, and identify both square roots.
  11. Avoid the sqrt(a)·sqrt(b) = sqrt(ab) pitfall for negatives — Recognise that the identity sqrt(a)·sqrt(b) = sqrt(ab) fails when both a and b are negative, and convert to i-form first.
  12. Apply algebraic identities to complex numbers — Use the standard algebraic identities, such as (z1 + z2)^2 and (z1 - z2)^3, which hold for all complex numbers, to expand powers.
  13. Compute the modulus |z| of a complex number — Compute the modulus of z = a + ib as the non-negative real number sqrt(a^2 + b^2).
  14. Form the conjugate of a complex number — Form the conjugate of z = a + ib by negating the imaginary part, giving a - ib.
  15. Use properties of modulus and conjugate over products and quotients — Apply the rules |z1 z2| = |z1||z2|, |z1/z2| = |z1|/|z2|, conjugate of a product/quotient/sum distributes, to simplify expressions.
  16. Represent a complex number as a point in the Argand plane — Plot z = x + iy as the point (x, y) in the complex (Argand) plane, with the modulus as its distance from the origin.
  17. Locate the conjugate as the mirror image across the real axis — See that the conjugate z = x - iy is the mirror image of z = x + iy in the real axis of the Argand plane.
  18. Reduce any complex expression to the standard form a + ib — Combine the algebra of complex numbers (add, multiply, powers of i, inverse, division, conjugate) to reduce any given expression to the standard form a + ib.
