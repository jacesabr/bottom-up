Statistics

This chapter of JEE Maths covers 14 concepts, taught bottom-up. The AI tutor builds each from the ground up and checks you understand it before the next. Here's what you'll work through:

  1. Explain why a measure of dispersion is needed beyond central tendency — Recognise that two data sets can share the same mean and median yet differ greatly in spread, so a single number describing scatter — a measure of dispersion — is required.
  2. Read the parts of a grouped (class-interval) frequency table — Identify the class interval, its lower and upper class limits, the class width h, the class mid-point, the frequency f_i, and the cumulative frequency of a grouped data table.
  3. Find the median of grouped data by the class-interval formula — Locate the median class using cumulative frequency, then apply M = l + ((N/2 − C)/f)·h to find the median of a continuous frequency distribution.
  4. Compute the range of a data set as maximum minus minimum — Find the range, the simplest measure of dispersion, as the difference between the largest and smallest observation, and note its limitation.
  5. Define mean deviation about a central value as the mean of absolute deviations — Define mean deviation about a value 'a' as the average of the absolute deviations |x_i − a|, and explain why absolute values are used.
  6. Compute mean deviation about mean and median for ungrouped data — Carry out the step-wise calculation of M.D.(x̄) and M.D.(M) for a raw list of observations.
  7. Compute mean deviation for a discrete frequency distribution — Weight each distinct value by its frequency to find M.D. about mean or median for a discrete frequency distribution.
  8. Compute mean deviation for a continuous frequency distribution — Replace each class by its mid-point (and find the median by the class formula) to compute mean deviation for grouped continuous data.
  9. Define variance as the mean of the squared deviations from the mean — Define variance σ² = (1/n)Σ(x_i − x̄)², explaining why squaring deviations and then averaging gives a proper measure of dispersion.
  10. Define standard deviation as the positive square root of the variance — Define σ = √(variance) and explain why taking the square root restores the original units of the data.
  11. Compute variance and standard deviation for frequency distributions — Apply the frequency-weighted variance/SD formulas to discrete and continuous (mid-point) frequency distributions, including the computational formula via Σf_i x_i².
  12. Find variance and SD by the step-deviation (short-cut) method — Use the change of origin and scale y_i = (x_i − A)/h to simplify large x_i, then recover σ via σ_x = h·σ_y.
  13. Predict how variance changes when observations are scaled or shifted — Use the rules that adding a constant leaves the variance unchanged and multiplying by k multiplies the variance by k².
  14. Choose and compute the right measure of dispersion to compare data sets — Given any data set (ungrouped, discrete or continuous), select and compute range, mean deviation, variance or standard deviation to quantify and compare scatter.
