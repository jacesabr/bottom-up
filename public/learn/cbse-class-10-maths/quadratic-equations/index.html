Quadratic Equations

This chapter of CBSE Class 10 Maths covers 19 concepts, taught bottom-up. The AI tutor builds each from the ground up and checks you understand it before the next. Here's what you'll work through:

  1. Solve a quadratic equation (find its roots) — Given a quadratic equation, find the values of x (its roots) that satisfy it, choosing factorisation, the quadratic formula, or discriminant analysis as the situation demands.
  2. Recognise a quadratic equation by its standard form — Test whether an equation, once simplified, has the form ax²+bx+c=0 with a≠0 — i.e. is a polynomial equation of degree 2.
  3. Rearrange an equation into standard form ax²+bx+c=0 — Move all terms to one side and collect like terms so the equation reads ax²+bx+c=0 in descending powers.
  4. Expand brackets and simplify before classifying — Multiply out products, squares and cubes of binomials and collect terms so the true degree of the equation becomes visible.
  5. Translate a word problem into a quadratic equation — Assign a variable, express the described quantities in terms of it, and form the equation that the situation imposes.
  6. Verify a value is a root by substitution — Substitute a candidate value into ax²+bx+c and confirm the result equals zero.
  7. Split the middle term to factorise a quadratic — Rewrite bx as a sum of two terms whose coefficients multiply to a·c and add to b, enabling factorisation by grouping.
  8. Factorise by grouping into two linear factors — After splitting the middle term, group the four terms in pairs, take common factors, and extract the shared binomial.
  9. Apply the zero-product property to a factored quadratic — From (linear)(linear)=0, conclude that at least one factor must be zero, giving two linear equations.
  10. Solve each linear factor to get a root — Solve each linear equation (factor = 0) for x to obtain the roots, including fractional roots.
  11. Recognise a repeated factor gives equal roots — When factorisation yields the same linear factor twice, report the single root as occurring twice (equal roots).
  12. Factorise a quadratic with surd coefficients — Split the middle term and factor by grouping when coefficients involve surds such as √6, √3, √2.
  13. Reject a root that the context forbids — In an applied problem, discard a mathematically valid root that violates a physical constraint (e.g. a negative length).
  14. Identify the coefficients a, b, c from standard form — Read off a, b and c — with correct signs — from a quadratic written as ax²+bx+c=0.
  15. Compute the discriminant b²–4ac — Evaluate b²–4ac with the correct signs to obtain the discriminant of a quadratic equation.
  16. Classify the nature of roots from the discriminant — Use the sign of b²–4ac to decide: two distinct real roots (>0), two equal real roots (=0), or no real roots (<0).
  17. Find a parameter k via a discriminant condition — Write the discriminant of a quadratic with an unknown coefficient k, impose the required condition (b²–4ac = 0 for equal roots, < 0 for no real roots), and solve for k.
  18. State the quadratic formula — Recall the formula x = (–b ± √(b²–4ac))/2a for the roots of ax²+bx+c=0.
  19. Apply the quadratic formula to find roots — Substitute a, b, c into x=(–b±√(b²–4ac))/2a, simplify the surd, and evaluate both roots.
