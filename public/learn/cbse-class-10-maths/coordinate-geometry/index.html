Coordinate Geometry

This chapter of CBSE Class 10 Maths covers 22 concepts, taught bottom-up. The AI tutor builds each from the ground up and checks you understand it before the next. Here's what you'll work through:

  1. Solve coordinate-geometry problems on points in the plane — Top skill: given coordinates of points, use the distance formula and the section formula to find distances, classify figures, locate points, and divide segments.
  2. Apply the distance formula to two points — Compute PQ = √[(x₂ − x₁)² + (y₂ − y₁)²] for points P(x₁,y₁) and Q(x₂,y₂).
  3. Compute coordinate differences with signed coordinates — Form x₂ – x₁ and y₂ – y₁ correctly when coordinates can be negative, e.g. (3 + 2) for 3 − (−2).
  4. Square the legs and sum them under the root — Square each difference and add: e.g. 5² + 5² = 50; (−4)² + (−6)² = 52.
  5. Find distance of a point from the origin — Special case OP = √(x² + y²) for the distance of P(x,y) from O(0,0).
  6. Classify a triangle from its three side lengths — Compute the three side lengths by distance; use the converse of Pythagoras (PQ²+PR²=QR²) to identify a right triangle, or equal sides for isosceles.
  7. Verify three points form a triangle — Three points form a triangle iff the sum of any two distances exceeds the third (not collinear).
  8. Show four points form a square via sides and diagonals — All four sides equal AND both diagonals equal ⇒ square.
  9. Test collinearity of three points by distances — Points A,B,C are collinear when AB + BC = AC.
  10. Set up an equidistant-point equation — Equidistant means AP = BP, so set AP² = BP² and expand.
  11. Derive the relation for a general equidistant point (x,y) — Expand AP²=BP² for (x,y) equidistant from two points and reduce to a linear relation like x − y = 2.
  12. Find a point on an axis equidistant from two points — A point on the y-axis is (0,y); set its squared distances equal and solve the single linear equation for y.
  13. Apply the section formula for internal division — P dividing \(A(x_1,y_1)\)–\(B(x_2,y_2)\) in ratio \(m_1:m_2\) internally is \(\left(\frac{m_1x_2+m_2x_1}{m_1+m_2}, \frac{m_1y_2+m_2y_1}{m_1+m_2}\right)\).
  14. Find the point dividing a segment in a given ratio — Plug concrete numbers into the section formula, e.g. ratio 3:1 of (4,−3) and (8,5) gives (7,3).
  15. Find the ratio in which a point divides a segment — Set the point's known coordinate equal to the section formula and solve for k (using k:1 form).
  16. Find the ratio in which an axis divides a segment — On the y-axis abscissa = 0, so set the x-section expression to 0 and solve for k.
  17. Find points of trisection of a segment — Trisection points divide AB in ratios 1:2 and 2:1; apply the section formula twice.
  18. Apply the midpoint formula — Midpoint of \(A(x_1,y_1)\), \(B(x_2,y_2)\) is \(((x_1+x_2)/2, (y_1+y_2)/2)\), the \(1:1\) special case of the section formula.
  19. Use diagonals-bisect property to find a parallelogram vertex — In a parallelogram the diagonals bisect each other, so midpoint of one diagonal = midpoint of the other; solve for the unknown.
  20. Apply the Pythagoras theorem to a right triangle — In a right triangle the hypotenuse squared equals the sum of the squares of the legs.
  21. Read and write a point's coordinates (abscissa, ordinate) — A point is (x, y): x is the abscissa (distance from y-axis), y the ordinate; axis points are (x,0) or (0,y).
  22. Equate ordered pairs componentwise — If (x,y) = (a,b) then x = a and y = b — split a coordinate equation into two scalar equations.
