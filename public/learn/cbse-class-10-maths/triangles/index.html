Triangles

This chapter of CBSE Class 10 Maths covers 22 concepts, taught bottom-up. The AI tutor builds each from the ground up and checks you understand it before the next. Here's what you'll work through:

  1. Prove triangles similar and apply similarity to solve problems — Use similarity criteria (AAA/AA, SSS, SAS) to prove two triangles similar, then use the proportional sides / equal angles to find unknown lengths, angles, or prove further results.
  2. Recognise similar figures (same shape, not necessarily same size) — State that two figures are similar if they have the same shape but not necessarily the same size, and that all congruent figures are similar but not conversely.
  3. State the two conditions for two polygons to be similar — Two polygons of the same number of sides are similar iff (i) corresponding angles are equal AND (ii) corresponding sides are in the same ratio.
  4. Know that one condition alone is insufficient for polygon similarity — Recognise via the square/rectangle and square/rhombus counterexamples that neither equal angles alone nor proportional sides alone make two polygons similar.
  5. Identify corresponding vertices, angles and sides between two figures — Match up vertices \(A' \leftrightarrow A, B' \leftrightarrow B,\) etc. and then the corresponding angles and corresponding sides so ratios and equalities are written between matching parts.
  6. Write a triangle similarity statement with correct vertex correspondence — Use the ~ symbol and order the vertices so that corresponding vertices match (Δ ABC ~ Δ DEF means A↔D, B↔E, C↔F).
  7. Apply the Basic Proportionality (Thales) Theorem to set up AD/DB = AE/EC — When a line is parallel to one side of a triangle and meets the other two sides, conclude the two sides are divided in the same ratio.
  8. Prove BPT using the equal-area argument — Join BE, CD, drop perpendiculars, express area ratios as AD/DB and AE/EC, and use that ar(BDE)=ar(DEC) (same base, same parallels) to conclude AD/DB = AE/EC.
  9. Apply the converse of BPT to conclude a line is parallel to a side — If a line divides two sides of a triangle in the same ratio, conclude that the line is parallel to the third side.
  10. Decide whether a line is parallel to a side by comparing computed ratios — Given numeric segment lengths, compute PE/EQ and PF/FR; if equal the segment is parallel to the third side, otherwise it is not.
  11. Transform a ratio equation by inverting and adding 1 — From AD/DB = AE/EC, invert to DB/AD = EC/AE, add 1 to both sides to get AB/AD = AC/AE, hence AD/AB = AE/AC.
  12. Apply the AAA / AA similarity criterion — If corresponding angles of two triangles are equal (or just two angles each), conclude the triangles are similar.
  13. Justify equal angles from parallel lines, vertically opposite, or common angles — Produce the equal-angle facts an AA proof needs: alternate angles and corresponding angles from parallels, vertically opposite angles, and a shared/common angle. (Using notation: ∠ P = ∠ S for alternate angles)
  14. Apply the SSS similarity criterion — If the three pairs of corresponding sides are in the same ratio, conclude the triangles are similar.
  15. Compute and compare the ratios of corresponding sides — Divide each pair of corresponding sides and simplify to check whether all the ratios are equal (e.g. \(\frac{3.8}{7.6} = \frac{1}{2}\)).
  16. Apply the SAS similarity criterion — If one angle of a triangle equals one angle of another and the sides including those angles are proportional, conclude the triangles are similar.
  17. Rearrange an equal-products relation into a side ratio — From OA · OB = OC · OD derive OA/OC = OD/OB so SAS can be applied.
  18. Read off equal angles and proportional sides from a stated similarity — Once Δ ABC ~ Δ PQR is established, write AB/PQ = BC/QR = CA/RP and ∠ A = ∠ P, ∠ B = ∠ Q, ∠ C = ∠ R.
  19. Find an unknown angle using similar triangles and the angle-sum property — Match the unknown angle to its equal corresponding angle in the similar triangle, then compute it (e.g. via 180° − ∠A − ∠B).
  20. Find an unknown length by solving the similarity proportion — Set up the equal-ratio equation from the similar triangles and solve for the unknown side (cross-multiply and isolate).
  21. Model a real-world height/shadow situation with similar triangles — Translate a lamp-post-and-shadow or pole-and-tower scenario into two similar right triangles sharing the sun/light angle, then use proportionality.
  22. Use medians (and the 2× relation) within similar triangles — Use AB = 2·AM and PQ = 2·PN to reduce side ratios to median-segment ratios, enabling SAS/SSS on the median sub-triangles.
