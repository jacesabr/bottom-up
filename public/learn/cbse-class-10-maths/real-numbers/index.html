Real Numbers

This chapter of CBSE Class 10 Maths covers 18 concepts, taught bottom-up. The AI tutor builds each from the ground up and checks you understand it before the next. Here's what you'll work through:

  1. Apply the Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic and irrationality results — Use prime factorisation, the Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic, and proof-by-contradiction to compute HCF/LCM, settle divisibility/ends-with-digit questions, and prove numbers irrational.
  2. Factorise a composite number into a product of primes — The factor-tree method breaks a composite into its prime building blocks — the fundamental step behind every HCF, LCM, and divisibility argument.
  3. State the Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic (existence + uniqueness) — Know that every composite number factorises as a product of primes, and that this factorisation is unique apart from order.
  4. Decide whether a power can end in a given digit using its prime factors — Argue that a number ends in 0 only if 5 (and 2) divide it, then check the prime factorisation for that prime.
  5. Compute HCF as the product of smallest powers of common primes — Take each prime common to all numbers and multiply its smallest occurring power to get the HCF.
  6. Compute LCM as the product of greatest powers of all primes — Take every prime appearing in any number and multiply its greatest occurring power to get the LCM.
  7. Use HCF(a,b) × LCM(a,b) = a × b to find the missing quantity — For two integers, the product of HCF and LCM equals the product of the numbers; rearrange to get LCM from HCF (or vice versa).
  8. State and apply Theorem 1.2: if a prime p divides a² then p divides a — Invoke the lemma that a prime dividing a perfect square must divide the base integer.
  9. Set up an irrationality proof by assuming the number is rational — Begin a proof by contradiction: assume the number equals a/b with a, b integers, b ≠ 0, and a, b coprime.
  10. Square and rearrange to get the divisibility relation — From b√n = a, square both sides to get n·b² = a², exposing that n divides a².
  11. Close the proof by exhibiting a common factor that contradicts coprimeness — Use Theorem 1.2 twice (on a, then on b) to show p divides both, contradicting that a, b are coprime.
  12. Prove (rational − irrational) is irrational by isolating the irrational — Assume the combination is rational, solve for the known irrational, and reach a contradiction.
  13. Prove (rational × irrational) is irrational by isolating the irrational — Assume the product is rational, divide out the rational factor to isolate the irrational, and reach a contradiction.
  14. Model a 'meet again' word problem as an LCM computation — Recognise that two periodic events coincide after a time equal to the LCM of their periods.
  15. Explain why an expression like n×m×...+k is composite by factoring out a common factor — Show a number is composite by exhibiting a non-trivial common factor across its summands.
  16. Recognise coprime integers (common factor only 1) and the prime/composite distinction — Coprime numbers share no common factor other than 1; factorisation targets composites and produces primes.
  17. Interpret the 'divides' relation between integers — The divisibility symbol p | a — 'p divides a' — is the basic shorthand the irrationality proofs translate into algebra at every step.
  18. Multiply and compare powers of primes — Two arithmetic skills underlie all HCF/LCM work: evaluating a prime power, and choosing the right exponent when the same prime appears in different numbers.
