Surface Areas and Volumes

This chapter of CBSE Class 10 Maths covers 24 concepts, taught bottom-up. The AI tutor builds each from the ground up and checks you understand it before the next. Here's what you'll work through:

  1. Find surface areas and volumes of combinations of solids — Real objects are built from two or more basic solids; to find their surface area or volume you must decompose the object and recombine the parts correctly.
  2. Decompose a combined solid into basic solids — Before applying any formula, name each constituent solid in the object and note where it is attached.
  3. Total surface area of a combination = sum of curved surface areas — When two solids are joined, the hidden flat faces vanish; total surface area = sum of exposed curved surfaces only.
  4. TSA of a cone surmounted by a hemisphere — A toy top (cone + hemisphere of equal radius) has total surface area = CSA hemisphere + CSA cone.
  5. Curved surface area of a hemisphere = 2πr² — The curved (dome) surface of a hemisphere is half the full sphere's surface: CSA = 2πr².
  6. Curved surface area of a cylinder = 2πrh — The side (lateral) surface of a cylinder wraps around once: CSA = 2πrh.
  7. Curved surface area of a cone = pirl — The slanted surface of a cone uses slant height l, not vertical height h: CSA = pirl.
  8. Compute slant height l = sqrt(r^2 + h^2) — A cone's slant height l is the hypotenuse of the right triangle formed by its radius r and vertical height h.
  9. Derive a part's height by subtracting from the total height — When only the total height of a combined solid is given, back out each part's height by subtraction.
  10. TSA of a cube with a hemisphere attached on a face — Attaching a hemisphere on a cube's face hides a circle but exposes a dome: TSA = 6a^2 - pir^2 + 2pir^2.
  11. TSA when a hemispherical depression is cut into a solid — A hemispherical cavity scooped into a cylinder's top adds the dome's inner curved surface to the cylinder's side.
  12. Account for an exposed base ring when stacked radii differ — When the cone's base is wider than the cylinder it sits on, the exposed annular ring must also be painted.
  13. Painted area of a cylinder part = CSA + one base circle — When one end of a cylinder is capped by another solid, the painted area is the side wall plus only the exposed bottom base.
  14. Total surface area of a solid hemisphere = 3pir^2 — A solid hemisphere has both the dome (2pir^2) and the flat circular base (pir^2), so TSA = 3pir^2.
  15. Volume of a combination = sum of the parts volumes — Joining solids hides surface area but not interior space; total volume is simply the sum of each part's volume.
  16. Volume of a cylinder = pir^2h — A cylinder's volume equals the area of its circular base times its height: V = pir^2h.
  17. Volume of a cone = (1/3)pir^2h — A cone holds exactly one-third of the cylinder with the same base and height: V = (1/3)pir^2h.
  18. Volume of a hemisphere = (2/3)pir^3 — A hemisphere holds half a sphere's volume: V = (2/3)pir^3.
  19. Volume of a half cylinder = (1/2)pir^2h — A half-cylinder (semicircular cross-section) has volume equal to half the full cylinder's volume.
  20. Volume of a cuboid = l x b x h — A cuboid (box) has volume equal to the product of its three dimensions: V = l x b x h.
  21. Actual capacity = apparent capacity minus volume of the raised part — A hemispherical bump at the base of a glass occupies space; actual capacity = cylinder volume minus hemisphere volume.
  22. Find volume difference between a circumscribing cylinder and the solid — The empty space around a toy inside its just-enclosing cylinder = cylinder volume minus toy volume.
  23. Keep units consistent before computing — When a problem mixes cm and m, convert everything to one unit first; then convert the final answer if needed.
  24. Use radius = diameter divided by 2 — Every area and volume formula in this chapter needs the radius; if a diameter is given, halve it first.
