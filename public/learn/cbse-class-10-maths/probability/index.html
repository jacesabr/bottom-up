Probability

This chapter of CBSE Class 10 Maths covers 16 concepts, taught bottom-up. The AI tutor builds each from the ground up and checks you understand it before the next. Here's what you'll work through:

  1. Compute the theoretical probability of an event — Combine favourable count and total count into P(E) = favourable/total, then reduce the fraction.
  2. Count the total number of equally likely outcomes — List every distinct outcome of the experiment; that count becomes the denominator of P(E).
  3. Count the outcomes favourable to an event — From the sample space, pick only those outcomes that make the event happen and count them.
  4. Form and reduce the probability fraction — Place favourable over total and divide both by their HCF to reach lowest terms.
  5. Decide whether outcomes are equally likely — Check for symmetry or fairness; only then is each outcome equally likely and the classical formula valid.
  6. Apply the complement rule P(not E) = 1 - P(E) — Subtract P(E) from 1 to find the probability of the opposite event without re-counting.
  7. Recognise an impossible event has probability 0 — When no outcome in the sample space satisfies the event, the favourable count is 0 so P(E) = 0.
  8. Recognise a sure event has probability 1 — When every outcome in the sample space satisfies the event, P(E) = total/total = 1.
  9. Apply the bound 0 ≤ P(E) ≤ 1 — Every probability must lie between 0 and 1 inclusive; any value outside this range is invalid.
  10. Use that elementary-event probabilities sum to 1 — The probabilities of all single-outcome (elementary) events of an experiment must add to exactly 1.
  11. List the sample space for two coins tossed together — Treat the two coins as distinct and list all four ordered pairs: (H,H),(H,T),(T,H),(T,T).
  12. List the sample space for two dice thrown together — Build the 6×6 grid of 36 ordered pairs (die1, die2) and use it to count event outcomes.
  13. Use the composition of a 52-card deck — Know: 52 cards total, 4 suits of 13, 4 aces, 12 face cards (K/Q/J), 26 red and 26 black.
  14. Compute probability as a ratio of lengths — For a continuous outcome on a line segment, P(E) = (length of favourable interval) / (total length).
  15. Compute probability as a ratio of areas — For a 2-D continuous outcome, P(E) = (favourable area) / (total area).
  16. Solve a word problem using the complement — When the direct event is hard to count, find the easier complementary event and subtract from 1.
