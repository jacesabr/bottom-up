The Teaching Graph

Start at the question, descend to first principles, climb back knowing.

After Plato’s Meno · c. 385 BCE

An old way of teaching was always missing one piece. Since 2024 that piece exists — and the whole thing becomes buildable. This is the thinking behind Sarthi.

Part One — The idea
I
The demonstration

A boy who knew no geometry, doubling a square

Good teaching draws the answer out of the learner. That it works was never in doubt.

In Plato’s Meno, Socrates asks an enslaved boy to double a square — area 4 into area 8. The boy guesses: double the side. That makes 16. He tries side 3 — that’s 9. Stuck, he finally admits he doesn’t know. Only then does Socrates lead him to the diagonal. The boy arrives at the answer; he is never handed it.

start · 4
side 4 · too big
side 3 · wrong
diagonal · 8

That is the method entire: begin from an outcome, find where the learner actually stands, then ask the questions that let them build the rest. Feynman reached the same shape from the other end — explain it plainly, watch where it breaks, go back, rebuild.

Socrates

Athens · 470–399 BCE
  • Question, don’t lecture
  • Begin from admitted ignorance
  • Draw the answer out of the learner
  • Let contradiction expose the gap

He called it the elenchus — questioning a confident answer into aporia, then, as midwife, drawing out the knowledge already there. The Meno’s slave boy is the proof.

Feynman

Caltech · 1918–1988
  • Explain it in plain language
  • Notice exactly where you stall
  • Return to the source material
  • Simplify, then build an analogy

His test: what I cannot create, I do not understand. His warning: you must not fool yourself — and a nod is usually just that.

A third stance names the product. Sārathi — the charioteer of the Bhagavad Gita who counsels and holds the reins, but never takes up the bow for you. Socrates asks; Feynman rebuilds; the sārathi guides. A map, not a script.
II
Why it never scaled

The method always worked. It couldn’t be staffed.

So why doesn’t every student have a tutor? Two limits — both human.

One-to-one teaching works extraordinarily well. Bloom’s 1984 shorthand: a tutored student beat about 98% of a normal classroom. He called it a problem — no society can afford a tutor for every child.

Honestly: that clean “two sigma” was never pure tutoring — Bloom’s students got tutoring plus mastery learning, and the figure rests on a thin pair of dissertations. Randomised trials since average nearer 0.37σ; the best human tutors reach about 0.8, strong computer tutors ~0.75. Tutoring isn’t magic — it just beats the lecture, reliably, and we could never give it to everyone.

The pedagogy was never the obstacle. Supply was — and supply fails two ways at once. Name them, because the rest of the argument is just closing them one at a time.

Gap I The bookkeeping

One question can fail in many places. Stuck on Newton’s second law? Could be force, mass, or acceleration — each with prerequisites of its own. One doubt fans into a branching tree, and holding which branch is open, where you paused, how deep you went, outruns memory fast.

Gap II The patience

Ask “make sense?” and you’ll get a yes. The nod isn’t a lie and isn’t understanding — it’s the wish to be done, to not look slow. Real teaching asks the next question anyway, past polite, past tired, until the gap shows. Humans run out of that patience.

III
Closing Gap I · the structure

Make the subject a graph

A graph fixes the bookkeeping. The idea is decades old — that’s the point.

Model knowledge not as a syllabus but as a graph: every concept a node, every edge pointing to a prerequisite. Now the branching tree of doubts isn’t held in anyone’s head — it’s the structure itself. You always know where you are and what you stand on.

This part isn’t novel, and it shouldn’t pretend to be. Knowledge Space Theory (1985) wired skills by prerequisite; ALEKS, built on it, has done exactly this commercially since the late 1990s — for millions of students a year.

So what stopped them? Those systems had to hide the graph and script the teaching — multiple-choice routing, fixed feedback — because the two things real tutoring needs did not yet exist: a partner that could hold an open conversation, and one that could see your work. Gap I was solvable all along. Gap II was not.
This closes Gap I — the bookkeeping
IV
How the graph runs

One kind of node, one loop

The same small machine drives a physics node or an art-history node.

The exam question is the root; first principles are the leaves — the irreducible forms a subject finally rests on. It goes as deep as the subject demands.

Every node carries the same four parts — which is what lets one engine drive any subject:

The loop: recursive descent

  1. Start at the rootPose the question, ask the learner to reason aloud.
  2. Run the dialogueA sound answer advances; a stall does not.
  3. On a stall, descendDrop to the missing prerequisite.
  4. Recurse to a leafA node with no prerequisites: a first principle.
  5. Cement, then ascendClear its exercises, return to the parent.
  6. Repeat to the rootEnd when the original question is answered.
V
Closing Gap II · what just changed

A machine that can talk, and see

The second limit needed a partner that never tires and an eye that reads your scratch work. Both arrived.

The tireless partner. A model can now hold an open Socratic dialogue — not route multiple-choice items, but ask the real next question, weigh a messy answer, and keep probing past where a person would tire or a student would rather move on. The patience Gap II demanded is, for the first time, in endless supply.

The eye. Learners don’t only type — they draw: a half-built diagram, an equation that trails off. A tutor glances and sees it — you’ve put the normal force where gravity goes. For most of computing history that glance was impossible; a messy sketch was noise to a machine. That was the last bottleneck.

This closes Gap II — the patience
VI
The conclusion

Both gaps are gone. What’s left is the work.

A proven method, finally buildable end to end — and honesty about what’s still hard.

The method works — that part was settled.

Two human limits kept it from everyone: bookkeeping, and patience.

The graph closed the first. Models that talk and see closed the second.

So a personal Socratic tutor is, for the first time, something you can build.

That is a conclusion, not a finish line. The architecture isn’t a discovery — the graph is decades old, the pedagogy millennia. What changed is that one system can now fill both gaps, cheaply, at any hour. The honest caveats stay in view:

Models still get things wrong. A confidently wrong tutor is worse than none.

Fake understanding fools the model too. The probing must be designed, not assumed.

Building the graphs is real labor. Good prerequisite structure is expert work.

Motivation isn’t in the architecture. A map doesn’t carry you; it keeps you from getting lost.

A teaching system should be a map, not a script — and the guide who walks it with you can finally be as patient as the method always asked.

Part Two · The build

So we started building it.

Sarthi turns the map into a tutor — for the exams where the stakes are highest.

VII
From idea to practice

Sarthi: the method, pointed at an exam hall

Everything above is the why. Sarthi is our attempt at the how.

We aimed it where the failure mode in Part One bites hardest: high-stakes Indian exam prep, where millions of students grind past papers while a single unspoken prerequisite gap quietly caps the score.

CBSEClass 10 & 12
JEEEngineering
NEETMedical

It is a direct translation of the architecture — and it is running. Each piece of the manifesto is now a piece of the product:

  • The graph → a per-subject prerequisite map: the exam’s own questions sit at the root — the spine the learner walks — and first principles at the leaves.
  • The node → each concept carries a teaching brain (its explanation, the moves to elicit, the usual misconceptions), one answer-checked gate, and edges to the concepts it stands on.
  • The loop → a cheap model runs the dialogue; on a gap a director routes across the whole graph and descends; on mastery it poses the gate, grades it, and teleports straight back up to the next exam question.
  • The eye → it reads a photo of handwritten working — transcribe first, then teach over what the student actually did. Non-negotiable for physics, chemistry, and math.
Live today. Four exams — CBSE Class 10 & 12, JEE, and NEET — about 5,900 concept nodes, served on free inference. The engine is content-agnostic: the brain is the authored graph, not the model — so adding an exam is a corpus problem, not a rewrite.

Underneath, six choices make up our approach. None is exotic; together they are the method, mechanised.

You sit the real paper

The spine of a session is an actual past exam — CBSE, JEE, NEET — question by question, in order. We don’t invent practice problems; the paper itself is the path. You attempt, and the tutor listens for where you stand.

When you stall, it goes down — not on

Get stuck and the tutor doesn’t just repeat the question louder. It asks what the question rests on, finds the prerequisite you’re actually missing — anywhere in that subject’s graph, not only one step back — and drops you there. The descent repeats until it reaches a first principle, a concept with nothing beneath it.

It checks by code where it can, by judgement only where it must

A multiple-choice or numeric answer is marked by a program — exact, instant, no model in the loop. Only a written answer goes to an LLM, and only as an examiner against the official marking scheme, told to fail on doubt. This is where our hardest open question lives: a confident nod can clear a recognition check without real understanding — the very illusion Socrates and Feynman hunted — so the live work is making the check itself demand reconstruction, not just recognition.

It reads your handwriting

Physics, chemistry and maths happen on paper. Photograph your working and the tutor transcribes it first, then teaches over what you actually wrote — a dropped sign, a step skipped — rather than a tidy retype. An eye on the scratch work was the last thing machines couldn’t do; now they can.

On mastery, you teleport straight back

Once the missing idea is solid, you don’t trudge back up through every step. You’re returned directly to the exam question that tripped you, to finish it with the gap closed — then on to the next question. The climb taught you; the return proves it transferred.

The engine is honest by construction

The “brain” is the authored graph, not the model — so the model is swappable, and adding an exam is a content problem, not a rewrite. An exam only goes live if it is fully built: every question mapped, the graph complete. Anything half-built fails closed and never serves. And nothing is called “working” without a check behind it — we trust measurements, not the model’s confidence.

VIII
The eye, in action

It reads what you actually wrote

For most of computing history a messy sketch was noise to a machine. That was the last bottleneck — and it just fell.

A tutor doesn’t need your work typed up neatly. It needs to see the half-finished line, the wrong turn, the arrow pointing the wrong way — and answer that. Modern vision models can. Photograph a page mid-attempt, however rough, and the tutor reads it and asks the next real question. Three attempts, on imaginary concept nodes:

For investors · what comes next
The hard part is the engine. Every exam after the first is a corpus.

The teaching engine commits to no subject and no exam — the “brain” is the authored graph, and the model is swappable behind it. So once the foundation is solid — the descent loop, the grading, the eye, and the pipeline that authors graphs — a new exam is a content project, not a rebuild. India’s big exams are the proving ground, not the ceiling.

Every high-stakes test on earth has the same shape: a fixed syllabus, a bank of past papers, and students who lose marks to a single hidden prerequisite gap. That is exactly what this engine is built to find and close — so expansion is a map of corpora to author, each one widening the market without touching the core.

Honest critique + build roadmap. Not part of the published piece — kept here so we don’t lose sight of it.
THREE LENSES (all contribute, on merit)
  Sarthi / sarathi  - the charioteer who counsels but never picks up the bow for
                      you. The guide stance: a map, not a script.
  Socrates          - draw the answer out by questioning; start from admitted
                      ignorance; the target is the illusion of knowledge.
  Feynman           - you understand it only if you can rebuild/explain it; the
                      first principle is you must not fool yourself.
  -> who the system is (guide) / how it teaches (question) / how it proves you
     learned (reconstruct). Keep them distinct.

THE HONEST CRITIQUE (where the names are / aren't earned)
  FAITHFUL: descent to the missing first principle = Feynman's "what I cannot
    create I do not understand," as control flow. Gate-must-pass + affect-never-
    descends = "don't fool yourself." The diagnosis is genuinely his.
  GAP 1 (biggest): the node EXPLAINS; Socrates/Feynman make the LEARNER explain.
    Today the student's job is to answer the gate, not reconstruct the idea.
  GAP 2: node mastery gates are mostly recognition (mcq/numeric/...). Recognition
    can be passed by elimination - the exact illusion both men hunted. The aligned
    gate (written/rubric, fail-on-doubt) is the minority case.
  GAP 3: telos is a credential (CBSE/JEE/NEET marks), not examined understanding.
    We borrow the FORM (descend to first principles) and bolt it to the cram goal.
    Truer self-description is our own README's: "a debugger for understanding."
  GAP 4: "first principle = node with no prereqs" is topology, not epistemics. A
    leaf is just where authoring stopped; only as good as the graph.

THE RESOLUTION
  Recognition is fine on the SPINE (it's literally the exam). Reconstruction is
  required at the CONCEPT NODES (that's where understanding is built). So change
  the NODE mastery gate to teach-back/explain; leave spine exam grading as-is.
  Targeted fix; respects both the credential and the understanding.

BUILD ORDER
  0. MEASURE FIRST (cheap). Tag every gate_pass with gate-kind + evidence quality;
     extend the admin audit to report "what did we accept as mastery?" Baseline.
  1. EXPLANATION AS THE NODE MASTERY GATE (contained, high-leverage). mastery =
     pass an explain/derive/teach-back (written, fail-on-doubt). mcq/numeric become
     confirmation, not the mastery proof. Touches: gate authoring (I-5), tester/
     longanswer rubric, reviewReadiness, mastery overlay. Mock can simulate it.
     Ship-able while the node still explains - strengthens "don't fool yourself"
     before the hard rework, and creates the forcing function for step 2.
  2. MAKE THE NODE SOCRATIC (the hard centerpiece). Extract node-agent teaching
     into an explicit MOVE policy: ASK_PROBE / ASK_PREDICT / ASK_EXPLAIN /
     COUNTEREXAMPLE / HINT / REVEAL / CONFIRM. Default to questioning; REVEAL only
     after K stuck turns or on request (bounded Socratic - mirror the force-pose
     backstop so it can't livelock). Add a `move` field to node-agent output
     (additive to the contract; orchestrator still reads `signal`). Needs a real-
     model Socratic eval (personas: confident-wrong / genuinely-stuck / fluent-
     restater); the mock covers wiring only. Per-node move counter needed.
  3. ANTI-GAMING + TELOS. Require a one-line "why" on mcq/numeric AT NODES (light
     rubric) so recognition alone can't certify a concept. Reframe metrics/product
     around reconstructable understanding; exam pass = side effect (true only
     after step 1).
  4. GRAPH EPISTEMICS (ongoing). Audit leaf nodes: real bedrock vs authoring
     stopped. Add a bedrock-review pass to the corpus pipeline.
  PARALLEL: manifesto rewrite - open on the three lenses converging on
     "understanding must be generated, not received"; cut the enslaved-boy story;
     promote the crystallography/descent figure to the hero.

KNOBS TO DECIDE (shape steps 1-2)
  - How bounded is Socratic? K questioning turns before a scaffolded reveal, under
    exam-time pressure.
  - Teach-back at every descended node, or only past a depth/importance threshold,
    to keep walk latency sane?
