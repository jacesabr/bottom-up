The idea

Start at the foundation. Build upward. The gap never forms.

Sarthi is built on an old, well-proven idea about how people actually learn — turned into a tutor that builds maths up from the bedrock, one gated concept at a time.

The two ways good teaching works

Draw it out, then rebuild it from first principles.

Two teachers, centuries apart, found the same thing.

Socrates
Don't lecture — ask. Question a confident answer until the gap is exposed, then draw the real understanding out of the learner.
Feynman
"What you cannot reconstruct, you do not understand." Find the bedrock idea and rebuild from there in plain words.
Sārathi
The charioteer of the Gita — counsels and holds the reins, but never picks up the bow for you. A guide, not a script.

That good teaching works was never in doubt — a strong tutor lifts a student roughly a full grade. The problem was only ever that it couldn't scale: one tutor, one student, far too expensive.

The problem it fixes

You didn't fail the hard thing.

You failed something simpler, a few chapters back, that nobody found. A missing prerequisite cascades: the integral feels impossible only because the derivative under it was never solid. Most "I'm just bad at maths" is really one hidden gap doing all the damage.

Sarthi's answer

Don't wait for the gap — build so it can't form.

Instead of dropping you into a hard question and digging down to the gap, Sarthi does the inverse: it builds you up from the foundations, in strict order. Every concept is taught, then a gate checks it — pass and the next opens; miss and it re-teaches, no failing out. You never reach an idea before the ideas it depends on, so nothing is ever assumed and the gap simply never appears.

Start at the bedrock. Master one concept. Let it unlock the next. Then sit one clean exam.
What that means in maths

The course is a prerequisite graph, climbed bottom-up.

Limits and derivatives come before continuity and integrals. Quadratics build on polynomials; the section formula is just solving a proportion. Every concept in CBSE 10, CBSE 12 and JEE maths is wired to the exact earlier ideas it rests on — and you climb that graph from the ground up, never out of order. The exam at the end isn't a hurdle; it's the proof that the whole structure holds.
