part one
the pitch
problem → why now → the method → what's actually built → the market → the ask.
the problem

students fail on something simpler, a few chapters back.

  • they don't fail the hard thing. they fail the easy thing underneath it — a prerequisite three chapters back nobody found.
  • tutoring fixes this — a good tutor is the highest-leverage help we know of — but it's unaffordable: coaching runs ₹1.5–5 lakh a year.
  • apps just throw more videos at you. more content isn't the gap; the missing prerequisite is.
why now

1:1 socratic tutoring stopped needing a human by the hour.

  • frontier models on the free NVIDIA NIM tier teach a concept and check you got it — at near-zero marginal cost per student (paid models only on fallback).
  • that's the unlock. it makes ₹1,000/month 1:1 coaching possible at all.
  • two years ago the cost floor made this impossible.
the method

don't skip ahead. build it up, gate every step.

  1. start at the foundation — the first concept in the chapter, nothing assumed.
  2. the ai teaches it — short socratic turns, ticking off each idea as you show it.
  3. a gate checks it — pass and the next concept opens; miss and it re-teaches. no failing out.
  4. climb the chapter, then the course — strictly in order, so you never meet an idea before its prerequisites.
  5. earn one clean exam — a real past paper, unlocked only after every concept is passed.

strict order, prerequisites wired, nothing assumed. the graph — not the chat — is the product.

the reward loop

pass a concept, earn a 10-minute game.

  • pass a concept, a short game unlocks — a hand-picked art game, under 10 minutes. a built-in break.
  • not candy: breaks are how you keep what you just learned. the game gives you a reason to stop before you hit a wall.
  • games unlock one per node, in order (eXit, Passage, A Dark Room, We Become What We Behold, …).
  • caveat: 18 games today — one per node for a course's opening stretch. more is content work, not a rebuild.

part two
questions you're going to ask
answered straight. i'd rather name the gaps than have you find them.
isn't this just a ChatGPT wrapper / another tutoring app?
no. the product isn't the chat — it's 823 prereq-wired nodes and gating that won't let you reach a concept before its basics. ChatGPT will teach the hard thing and let you fail the easy thing under it. the graph is the work; a wrapper can't fake it.
what does a student actually cost you? (unit economics)
~₹0 today — free NIM tier, paid models only as fallback. so margins are high. caveat: that's at low volume; at scale the number to watch is the paid-fallback rate. no CLV:CAC yet — i haven't earned one.
how do you acquire students — what's CAC?
none yet — pre-launch. plan: free product → word of mouth in exam-prep communities → measure. i won't quote a CAC i haven't paid.
why would they stay — retention?
none yet either. the bet's built into the loop: 1) you always have exactly one next concept open — never a blank wall; 2) each pass earns a 10-minute game — a reward and a break when focus fades. to prove with real users. milestone #1 of the raise.
why won't Byju's / Khan / Google just do this?
they could. the real alternatives today are coaching centres (₹1.5–5L), free YouTube, and Byju's / Physics Wallah / Khan. what's hard to copy: the 823-node prerequisite graph + strict gating — a generic LLM tutor won't refuse to teach the next thing until the basics are passed — plus a price they aren't built to hit. a head start, not a forever moat. i won't pretend it's one.
if it works, what does the world look like in 3–5 years?
an Indian kid preps for a maths exam with a ₹1,000/month tutor that finds the gap — not a ₹2-lakh coaching seat. then physics, chemistry, SAT. one method, many subjects.
what could kill this?
three real ones: 1) the gating feels like a cage and students bounce — unproven till real use. 2) the free NIM tier changes terms or quality and the economics shift. 3) distribution — building it was easy; getting it to students isn't solved. the raise targets #1 and #3.
who's building it, and what exactly does the raise buy?
who's building it: see the team section above — Suhas & Jace, ~1,620 nodes shipped across two live products. exact ask amount + runway are covered live (no numbers here i can't stand behind). what the money does is fixed: a lean team to get sarthi from built to verified with real students.
part three
where it goes next
the engine doesn't care what subject it teaches. content is data poured into the same graph.
the pivot isn't hypothetical  verified · live

we already shipped a second one — a totally different field.

  • the same engine runs IE — Inference Engineering: a graduate-level course on how LLMs actually run — transformers, KV cache, FlashAttention, quantization, vLLM, parallelism. live at ie-web.onrender.com.
  • 797 concept nodes across 55 chapters, same prerequisite-wired graph, same teach-then-gate loop, same ~₹0 NIM inference, same game-on-unlock — zero engine changes, just different content.
  • the thesis in one fact: maths for a 15-year-old and LLM internals for an engineer are the same product, different data.
so the real market isn't “maths exams.” it's anything you can put into a human brain — any field with prerequisites, however hard it looks for an AI: from nothing to advanced physicist, chemist, brain surgeon. every subject, every exam, every trade. IE proves the engine doesn't care which — it already teaches one as hard as LLM internals.

  • next: physics and chemistry (the JEE/NEET trio with maths), then the wider science & board set.

  • the same build-up engine runs SAT (maths first, the natural bridge), then the AP / ACT / standardized-test family.
  • different syllabus, different past papers — same core: concepts, prerequisites, strict order, earn-the-exam.
  • so the market in part one is the wedge, not the ceiling: India maths-exam → all exam subjects → other countries' exams.
physics/chem and SAT are designed, not built — roadmap the engine supports, not shipped. proven on maths; extending it is content work. i'll call it built when the graphs exist.
part four
the long view
the bet behind all of it — clearly marked: this is vision, not roadmap.
the belief

passive absorption is over. brick-and-mortar schooling ends.

  • one-way absorption in rows is a soon-to-be relic — looked back on like cavemen drawing on rocks to pass knowledge down.
  • textbooks and lecture halls end up behind glass — museum pieces of “how learning used to work.”
  • what replaces it: active, high-stimulation learning — talking it out, drawing your answer, teaching it back, caught and corrected where you're weak. not watching and re-watching.
the room

a seat, a 180° screen, and a tutor that knows where you left off.

  • a student walks into a small room — a seat and a monitor that fills their entire front field of vision.
  • the AI says hello and asks what they want to study today, then picks up exactly where they left off.
  • it talks through concepts, draws visuals on the fly in different ways, and asks the student to teach it back.
  • it finds where they're weak, corrects them, and routes the journey — the build-up + gating engine, with voice and visuals on top.
hands-on, and beyond

even physical experiments. even the trades. eventually the robots.

  • physical experiments: the AI guides the student through a delivered kit of materials — the same teach → check → correct loop, run in the real world.
  • trade schools stay the last truly hands-on, person-to-person learning — until robots can do the hands-on part.
  • and when they can, humans get trained to guide and control those robots — through this same learning system. the loop closes on itself.
hard framing: all of part four is vision — designed, not built. the room, the voice tutor, the visuals, the kits, the robots — none of it is shipped. what's real today is the engine under it (parts one–three): two courses in two unrelated fields, prereq-wired, gated, at ~₹0. the vision is why it's worth funding; the engine is what's proven.
build from the ground up. gate every step. earn the exam. — sarthi
