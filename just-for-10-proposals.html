Inference: the recurring meeting
Inference is the recurring meeting between a one-time-trained, frozen model and a real world that pushes back on three distinct axes (hardware physics, unit economics, shipping politics) — and most of the engineering difficulty lives there, after training, not in training.
BEFORE — key moves (4)
  1. The trained model is a fixed asset; inference is the recurring meeting between that asset and a reality that changes on three axes
  2. Physics (one GPU's bytes/compute) → economics (a thousand users' cost) → politics (a deadline's good-enough-on-time) are distinct pressures, not one scaled up
  3. These three pressures prefigure the book's three rings: runtime, infrastructure, tooling
  4. The larger share of the magic — cost, latency, and real-world difficulty — lives after training, in inference, not in training itself
AFTER — key moves (4)
  1. A trained model is a finished, unchanging thing built once; inference is the moment that finished thing is run on a new request to produce an output — and that moment repeats for every single request, for the whole life of the product.
  2. "Making inference work" is not one problem but three different forces pushing back at once: the hard limits of a single piece of computing hardware, the same tiny per-request cost multiplied across a flood of users, and the pressure to ship something good-enough on time instead of perfect-but-late.
  3. Those three forces are different in kind, not just different in size — a change that wins on one (say, cheaper per request) can lose on another (say, slower) — so they can never all be maximized at once and must be traded off.
  4. The learner can defend the surprising claim that most of the real difficulty, cost, and slowness of an AI product lives after training, in this repeated running step — not in the one-time training that gets all the attention.

Serving, not training, is the product
Serving (inference), not training, is the product: training is a one-time capital cost while inference is a recurring per-request cost paid for the product's whole life, so cumulative spend AND most engineering difficulty land after training — and how you serve the weights, not the weights themselves, decides whether the product is fast enough, cheap enough, and even physically possible.
BEFORE — key moves (4)
  1. Training is a one-time capital cost; inference is a recurring per-request cost paid for the life of the product, so cumulative spend lands after training
  2. Inference is as mathematical as training — the math is just about bytes moved, arithmetic intensity, cache hits, and tail latencies
  3. The same weights can be a viable product or an impossible one depending purely on how they are served
  4. Usability, affordability, and feasibility are all settled at inference time, not at training time
AFTER — key moves (4)
  1. Training is a one-time cost you pay once to build the model; serving (inference) is a small cost paid again on every single request, for the whole life of the product — so because the requests never stop and vastly outnumber the training runs, the total money and most of the engineering trouble pile up AFTER training, not during it.
  2. Serving is just as much a math problem as training, but a DIFFERENT kind of math problem: training's effort is about teaching the model, while serving's effort is about how fast the fixed model's numbers can be fetched from the GPU's memory and turned into an answer — a different set of quantities to worry about.
  3. The exact same trained model can be a usable product or an impossible one depending ONLY on how it is served — same numbers, same GPU, but the right serving makes it many times faster and cheaper than the wrong serving.
  4. Whether the product is fast enough, cheap enough, or even physically possible at all is decided at serving time (how you run it), not at training time (how you built it).

What attention does
What attention does in a language model: it assigns each earlier token a relevance weight so the model can focus on the tokens most relevant to the one it is generating now — and computing those weights costs N×N (quadratic in context length).
BEFORE — key moves (3)
  1. Attention assigns a relevance weight to each earlier token — deciding what to focus on when generating the next one
  2. Computing attention requires N × N comparisons for an N-token context — cost grows as the square of context length
  3. This quadratic scaling is why attention is one of the hardest and most engineered components in the inference stack
AFTER — key moves (3)
  1. Raw words arrive context-free, so to choose the next word the model must pull in the few earlier words that actually matter (like leaning on 'engineer' and 'her' to finish '...handed in her ___') rather than weighing every earlier word equally — and attention is the mechanism that does that selecting.
  2. Attention gives every earlier word a relevance weight — a number saying how much the current word should care about it right now — high for related words, near-zero for unrelated ones, then blends in mostly the high-weight words; and it re-decides these weights fresh for every single word the model generates.
  3. Because every position has to be compared against every other position, an N-word context takes N×N comparisons (1,000 words → a million; 100,000 → ten billion) — so doubling the context roughly quadruples the work, and this growing-as-the-square cost is why making attention cheaper is one of the biggest jobs in serving a model.

What a number format is
What a number format is: the bit-width and layout used to store each model weight, which fixes the precision/memory/speed tradeoff (FP32 / FP16 / BF16 / INT8, and quantization).
BEFORE — key moves (3)
  1. Model weights are numbers; the number format (FP32 / FP16 / INT8) determines how many bits store each weight
  2. Halving the bit-width halves the memory: a 7B model goes from 28 GB (FP32) to 14 GB (FP16) to 7 GB (INT8)
  3. Quantization is the deliberate choice to accept less precision in exchange for lower memory and faster arithmetic
AFTER — key moves (5)
  1. A trained model is just a giant list of numbers (its weights), and every one of those numbers has to be loaded into GPU memory before any inference can run — so if the list doesn't fit in memory, the model can't run at all.
  2. The number format is the rule for how many bits are spent storing each weight, and that bit-count directly sets the model's memory size: bytes-per-weight times the number of weights — so halving the bits halves the memory (a 7B model: ~28 GB → ~14 GB → ~7 GB).
  3. Fewer bits also tends to make inference faster, for two separate reasons: there are fewer bytes to move out of memory each step, and the smaller numbers are quicker to do arithmetic on.
  4. Spending fewer bits means each weight is rounded to one of fewer possible values, so you lose some precision — but for most weights that rounding barely changes the output, which is why trading precision for memory and speed is usually a good deal.
  5. Quantization is the name for deliberately making that trade — re-storing the weights in a smaller format to shrink memory and speed up serving, accepting a small, usually unnoticeable rounding cost — and picking the format is one of the engineer's first decisions.

What the KV cache is
The KV cache: storing each token's attention key and value vectors once (at prefill) and reusing them on every later decode step, turning the per-step recomputation of all past tokens' K/V from O(N) into an O(1) lookup (O(N) total instead of O(N^2)) — paid for in GPU memory that grows linearly with context length.
BEFORE — key moves (5)
  1. Without a KV cache the model recomputes keys and values for all previous tokens on every generation step — O(N²) total work; K/V from past tokens are immutable, so recomputing them is pure redundancy
  2. The KV cache stores each token's key/value on first compute (during prefill) and reuses/extends them on every decode step — turning recomputation into O(1)-per-step, O(N)-total lookup; ~1000× fewer FLOPs at N=1000
  3. On the roofline the dot lifts: bytes fall even faster than FLOPs, so arithmetic intensity climbs toward the ridge
  4. The cache costs GPU memory proportional to context length and lives in VRAM by default; the KV-cache budget limits simultaneous users and maximum context, and concurrent requests multiply the footprint
  5. Caching is not a pure speedup: it relocates cost from compute to memory, and that new memory cost becomes the next villain the runtime stack must fight
AFTER — key moves (5)
  1. Each earlier token, to be attended to, gets turned into two pieces of information: one that says 'what am I about' (so the current token can match against it) and one that says 'what do I carry' (the information it contributes). These two pieces depend only on that token and the ones before it, so once a token is in the past they never change — which means recomputing them on a later step always gives the exact same numbers and is pure wasted work.
  2. Without storing them, generating a long answer redoes this work for every past token on every single step: for a 1,000-token output the very first token's two pieces get rebuilt almost 1,000 times. Because each new step re-touches all the tokens so far, the total work grows as the square of the length (the same square-of-context blowup attention already has) — for a 10,000-token answer that is about 50 million attention computations instead of 10,000.
  3. The fix is to compute each token's two pieces once, the first time, and keep them in a store that you look up and add one new pair to on every later step. That turns each step's repeated work from 'redo every past token' into 'just one lookup plus one new pair,' so the total work over the whole answer grows in step with the length instead of with its square — the single biggest 'free lunch' in serving a model.
  4. The saving is not free: the stored pieces sit in the same GPU memory the weights need, and the store grows steadily as the conversation gets longer — for a very long context it can swell to take up more room than the model's own weights. Every separate user being served at the same time keeps their own store, so this memory budget is what actually caps how many users you can serve at once and how long each one's context can be.
  5. So the store does not make the cost disappear — it moves it: you stop paying in repeated computation and start paying in GPU memory that climbs with every token and every extra simultaneous user. That new memory pressure becomes the next problem the rest of the serving machinery is built to fight.
